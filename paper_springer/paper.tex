\begin{abstract}
We present a hybrid deep learning framework for forecasting next-day realized volatility across 14 stocks on the National Stock Exchange of India. Our Regime-Aware Temporal Fusion Transformer (RA-TFT) integrates high-frequency 5-minute data ($\sim$2.65M observations), HMM-derived market regimes, DCC-GARCH cross-asset correlations, and GARCH(1,1) conditional volatility into an interpretable attention-based architecture. An ablation study comparing the model with autoregressive input (Model~A, $R^2{=}0.976$) and without (Model~B, $R^2{=}0.258$) quantifies the contribution of volatility persistence versus genuine feature-based prediction. The TFT's variable selection networks reveal that lagged daily realized volatility dominates feature importance, confirming the HAR-RV structure, while regime-specific analysis shows performance degradation during extreme volatility periods.

\keywords{Volatility Forecasting \and Temporal Fusion Transformer \and Hidden Markov Model \and GARCH \and Deep Learning}
\end{abstract}

%===================================================================
\section{Introduction}
%===================================================================

Volatility forecasting is central to financial risk management, option pricing, and portfolio optimization. Traditional approaches---GARCH family models~\cite{bollerslev1986} and the Heterogeneous Autoregressive model of Realized Volatility (HAR-RV)~\cite{corsi2009}---have well-known limitations: GARCH assumes parametric conditional distributions and is restricted to univariate settings, while HAR-RV captures long memory but assumes linear dynamics. Deep learning architectures (LSTMs, Transformers) offer non-linear modeling capacity but typically lack interpretability---a critical requirement in finance.

We propose a \textbf{Regime-Aware Temporal Fusion Transformer (RA-TFT)} that addresses these gaps by integrating: (i)~HMM-derived market regime labels, (ii)~DCC-GARCH cross-asset correlations, (iii)~GARCH(1,1) conditional volatility, and (iv)~HAR-RV lag features into the TFT architecture~\cite{lim2021}. The TFT's built-in Variable Selection Networks (VSN) and multi-head attention provide model-intrinsic interpretability.

Our contributions include: (1)~multi-asset learning across 14 NSE stocks; (2)~regime-aware conditioning via HMM states; (3)~a transparent ablation study disentangling autoregressive persistence from feature-based prediction; (4)~interpretability analysis of learned feature importance and attention patterns; and (5)~first comprehensive TFT-based intraday volatility study on Indian equity markets.

\section{Data and Feature Engineering}
%===================================================================

\subsection{Dataset}
We use 5-minute OHLCV data for 14 liquid NSE stocks spanning $\sim$10 years: RELIANCE, ADANIPORTS, COALINDIA, HDFCBANK, ICICIBANK, AXISBANK, SBIN, INFY, TCS, WIPRO, LT, HAL, TATASTEEL, and TITAN. Indian market hours (9:15~AM--3:30~PM IST) produce 75 bars per trading day, yielding $\sim$2.65 million total observations.

\subsection{Features (47 total)}
Features are organized into six categories:

\noindent\textbf{Realized Volatility} (multi-scale): $RV_h = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{h} r_i^2}$ computed over 12 (1h), 38 (half-day), 75 (1d), and 375 (1w) bars, annualized by $\times\sqrt{252}$.

\noindent\textbf{HAR-RV Lags}: Lagged RV at 1-bar, 1-hour, 1-day, and 1-week intervals following Corsi~\cite{corsi2009}.

\noindent\textbf{Jump Detection}: Bipower variation, jump component, and jump ratio based on Barndorff-Nielsen \& Shephard~\cite{bns2004}.

\noindent\textbf{Range Estimators}: Parkinson~\cite{parkinson1980}, Garman-Klass~\cite{garman1980}, Rogers-Satchell~\cite{rogers1991}.

\noindent\textbf{Cross-Asset}: DCC-GARCH proxy correlation with equal-weighted market return. \textbf{GARCH}: Per-stock GARCH(1,1)~\cite{bollerslev1986} conditional volatility.

\noindent\textbf{Temporal}: Cyclical encodings ($\sin$/$\cos$) for hour, day-of-week, month; session flags (\texttt{is\_opening}, \texttt{is\_closing}).

\subsection{Target Variable}
The target is next-day realized volatility (75-bar forward):
\begin{equation}
y_t = \log\!\Big(1 + \sqrt{\textstyle\sum_{i=t+1}^{t+75} r_i^2} \times \sqrt{252}\Big)
\end{equation}
using \emph{only future bars}, ensuring zero overlap with backward-looking features.

\subsection{Regime Detection}
A 4-state Gaussian HMM~\cite{hamilton1989} is trained on market-average log-returns and daily RV, producing states: Low $\to$ Normal $\to$ High $\to$ Extreme volatility.

%===================================================================

\subsection{Ablation Study Design}
To disentangle volatility persistence from genuine predictive power:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Model~A} (with AR): includes $y_{t-1}$ as input, analogous to HAR-RV.
\item \textbf{Model~B} (without AR): excludes the target, testing pure feature-based prediction.
\end{itemize}
Consecutive daily RV targets share 74/75 bars (98.7\% overlap, lag-1 autocorrelation 0.9963). Model~A exploits this persistence; Model~B must rely solely on backward-looking features.

%===================================================================
\section{Baselines}
%===================================================================

We compare against: (i)~\textbf{GARCH family} (GARCH(1,1), EGARCH, GJR-GARCH) fitted per stock with Student-t innovations, best model selected by AIC; and (ii)~\textbf{HAR-RV}~\cite{corsi2009} using OLS with Newey-West standard errors. HAR-RV achieves $R^2 > 0.98$ for all stocks, establishing a strong linear baseline.

Model~B ($R^2{=}0.258$) demonstrates modest but non-trivial predictive power from backward-looking features alone. The 71.8pp gap quantifies the autoregressive contribution.

Directional accuracy near 50\% for both models indicates that predicting the \emph{direction} of RV changes is harder than predicting levels---consistent with near-random-walk dynamics in log-RV.

\subsection{Per-Stock Heterogeneity}
Model~B performance varies substantially: ADANIPORTS achieves the best $R^2{=}0.503$, while SBIN yields $R^2{=}{-}1.525$. Stocks with higher Model~B $R^2$ (ADANIPORTS, TATASTEEL, WIPRO) tend to have more predictable volatility from exogenous features, while banking stocks show weaker feature-based predictability.

\subsection{Interpretability}
The TFT's Variable Selection Network reveals encoder feature importance (Fig.~\ref{fig:importance}). \texttt{RV\_1d\_lag75} (1-day lagged RV) dominates with importance 60.4---\emph{two orders of magnitude} above all others---confirming the TFT learns a HAR-RV-like autoregressive structure. Secondary contributors include \texttt{RV\_1w} (0.23), \texttt{market\_correlation} (0.13), and \texttt{garch\_volatility} (0.12).

The decoder weights \texttt{is\_opening} highest (32.3), consistent with the documented opening auction effect in Indian markets where significant price discovery occurs at market open.

\section{Data Integrity}
%===================================================================

We verify: (i)~the target uses \emph{only future bars}, ensuring zero feature-target overlap; (ii)~train/validation split is strictly temporal (no random shuffling); (iii)~the 98.7\% consecutive overlap in rolling RV targets is a natural property of rolling-window estimators (not leakage), transparently addressed via the ablation study.

%===================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%===================================================================

We presented a Regime-Aware Temporal Fusion Transformer for next-day RV forecasting across 14 Indian stocks ($\sim$2.65M observations). Key findings: (1)~Model~A ($R^2{=}0.976$) matches HAR-RV baselines, confirming effective learning of volatility persistence; (2)~Model~B ($R^2{=}0.258$) demonstrates non-trivial feature-based predictive power; (3)~regime analysis reveals strong performance in calm markets ($R^2{>}0.98$) but degradation during crises; (4)~\texttt{RV\_1d\_lag75} dominates feature importance (60.4 vs.\ ${<}0.3$ for others), confirming HAR-RV structure.

Future work includes regime-specific model ensembles for tail events, multi-horizon prediction, Diebold-Mariano significance tests, and deployment in real-time trading systems.
